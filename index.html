The Two Ideas

The Hypothesis

Crossing the stars need not make you good at everything

It starts from a constraint. What if UFOs don't do much beyond travel, and don't scale well? What if an alien race happened to discover a physics unknown to us, yet isn't technologically superior to us in every way?

A visitor like that could never ship a living colony across the stars. The question is what it would do instead.

The Equation

A clock for the day we learn to grow a body

The Kent Equation estimates K(t), the probability that by a given year a civilization can turn genetic information into a living, viable body on its own world.

The hypothesis calls the threshold where this becomes possible the bootstrap point. Writing a genome is the fast part; growing it into a living body is the slow one, and that is what sets the date.

Those constraints, and the looming bootstrap point in our near future, reveal a unique perspective on UFO and alien research over the last eighty years.

Both models are speculative and conditional. They do not claim that extraterrestrial visitation is real; they ask what would follow if a specific set of premises held. The Kent Equation also stands on its own as a question about us: when a planet first learns to grow a body to specification, whether or not anyone is waiting to use that power.
